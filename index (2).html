The EU AI Act arrives in August. What actually changes for localisation?

On 2 August 2026, the bulk of the EU AI Act becomes applicable. If you work in localisation — buying it, selling it, or building the technology behind it — you've probably heard everything from “this changes nothing” to “this changes everything.” As usual, the truth is more boring, and more useful.

First, the good news: most translation and localisation AI sits in the Act's minimal- or limited-risk categories. Machine translation, AI dubbing, subtitle generation — none of it is “high-risk” in the regulatory sense, and the genuinely heavy obligations (conformity assessments, CE marking, EU database registration) don't apply to a typical localisation stack.

What does apply is transparency. From August, providers of generative AI systems must ensure their outputs are identifiable as AI-generated, and certain content must be clearly labelled. May's “AI Omnibus” amendments gave systems already on the market a short extension — to 2 December 2026 — for machine-readable marking, but the direction of travel is set: if your workflow produces synthetic voices, AI-dubbed video or machine-generated text at scale, marking and disclosure are becoming table stakes.

The omnibus also pushed the high-risk deadlines out, to December 2027 for stand-alone systems and August 2028 for AI embedded in regulated products. Some read that as a reprieve. I read it as a signal that the supporting standards and guidance are still being finished — not that anyone gets to stop preparing.

If you're a buyer, three practical questions to put to your vendors now: How will your outputs meet the marking and labelling requirements? Under the Act, are you the provider or the deployer for this workflow — and which are we? And how do our human-in-the-loop steps interact with the disclosure obligations? The vendors with clear answers are the ones treating compliance as product, not paperwork.
